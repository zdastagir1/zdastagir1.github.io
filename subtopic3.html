Subtopic 3: Technology in Courts & Corrections

Source 1

Field Information
URL https://www.amu.apus.edu/area-of-study/criminal-justice/resources/technology-in-criminal-justice/
Site Author American Military University (AMU)
Abstract This article from American Military University provides a broad overview of how technology is being used throughout the criminal justice system, with a focus on courts and corrections. In courts, the article explains how electronic case management systems help judges and legal staff handle large workloads more efficiently. Technology tools like 3D models and projectors help make evidence easier to understand for juries. In the corrections system, the article describes how prisons use biometric systems and inmate monitoring tools to maintain security and safety. GPS-enabled ankle monitors allow probation and parole officers to track offenders in the community. Digital learning platforms and telemedicine are also being used to provide education and mental health services to incarcerated individuals, which can help reduce the likelihood that someone will reoffend after being released. The article also looks ahead to future technologies such as AI, blockchain for evidence management, and virtual reality for crime scene recreation.

Source 2

Field Information
URL https://www.mackinac.org/virtual-justice-using-new-technology-in-the-criminal-justice-system
Site Author Mackinac Center for Public Policy
Abstract This article from the Mackinac Center for Public Policy examines both the benefits and drawbacks of using technology in courts and corrections. The article highlights the Matterhorn Court Innovations project as a positive example, which allows courts to handle minor civil infractions and ticket disputes entirely online. In Washtenaw County, Michigan, this system reduced the average duration of informal hearings from over two and a half hours down to just 27 minutes. The article also notes that the system helped reduce unnecessary arrests and saved thousands of hours of work for court and law enforcement staff. However, the article also raises concerns about the growing use of video visitation technology in prisons. While video calls can help reduce travel for families visiting loved ones, many facilities have completely eliminated in-person visits in favor of screens, which critics argue harms the relationships between inmates and their families. The article concludes that technology works best in the justice system when it is used carefully and in situations where human interaction is not essential.
