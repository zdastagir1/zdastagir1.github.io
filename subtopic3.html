Subtopic 3: Technology in Courts & Corrections

Source 1

Field Information
URL https://www.amu.apus.edu/area-of-study/criminal-justice/resources/technology-in-criminal-justice/
Site Author American Military University (AMU)
Abstract This article by American Military University presents an overall view of how the criminal justice system uses technology, including courts and corrections. It shows that court managers and other legal personnel can process high volumes of paperwork using Electronic Case Management Systems (ECMS). In addition, ECMS allows judges and attorneys to review information at their desks. Also, ECMS reduces the need for paper files because all records are stored electronically. In the courtroom, ECMS has increased efficiency and productivity by allowing judges, prosecutors, defense counsel, clerks and other legal personnel to access information quickly. Courts have been able to increase public safety and confidence in the administration of justice through this efficient use of technology. The article also points out that many courts now have 3-D images and/or video projectors available to assist jurors in understanding complex evidence. Jurors are no longer required to rely solely on oral testimony or written descriptions. In corrections, prisons have developed new methods of maintaining order and safety, including biometric systems and inmate monitoring tools. Probation and Parole Officers have begun to use GPS enabled ankle bracelets to monitor offenders in the community. Digital learning platforms and telemedicine are also becoming increasingly popular to support inmate education and mental health services, which helps decrease recidivism rates once inmates are released back into society.

Source 2

Field Information
URL https://www.mackinac.org/virtual-justice-using-new-technology-in-the-criminal-justice-system
Site Author Mackinac Center for Public Policy
Abstract This article by the Mackinac Center for Public Policy explores the advantages and disadvantages of implementing technology into Courts and Corrections. It uses an example called the Matterhorn Court Innovations Project; which provides all aspects of processing Minor Civil Infractions, Ticket Disputes etc., through the web. For instance, Washtenaw County, MI, had the average time of informal hearings with technology at approximately 27 minutes. They were able to save thousands of hours of work by Courts and Law Enforcement officials and to greatly minimize the number of unnecessary arrests. On the other hand, while it has been stated that video visitation technology will provide great benefit to family members that have to travel long distances to see their loved ones who are incarcerated, some facilities are moving away from in person visits and instead using a screen. Many advocates state that these types of technologies harm inmate's relationship with their families. Overall, the article states that technology is most effective in the Justice System when implemented in careful fashion and only in areas where Human Interaction is not necessary.
