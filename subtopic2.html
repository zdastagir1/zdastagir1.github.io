Subtopic 2: Predictive Policing & AI

Source 1

Field Information
URL https://naacp.org/resources/artificial-intelligence-predictive-policing-issue-brief
Site Author NAACP (National Association for the Advancement of Colored People)
Abstract This issue brief from the NAACP examines the use of artificial intelligence in predictive policing and the serious concerns it raises around racial bias and civil rights. Predictive policing refers to software that uses past crime data and algorithms to forecast where crimes may occur and who might commit them. The NAACP argues that because these systems rely on historical data from law enforcement, they can reflect and reinforce racial biases that already exist in the criminal justice system. The brief notes that the Black community is disproportionately impacted by over-policing, and that predictive tools can make this problem worse rather than better. Several U.S. senators have also called on the Department of Justice to stop funding such systems until proper audits and due process protections are in place. The NAACP recommends that states require transparency about how these tools work, ban the use of biased data, and involve communities in decisions about deploying AI in law enforcement.

Source 2

Field Information
URL https://www.brennancenter.org/our-work/research-reports/dangers-unregulated-ai-policing
Site Author Brennan Center for Justice
Abstract This report from the Brennan Center for Justice explores the growing use of AI-powered data fusion tools by police departments and the dangers that come with using these technologies without proper oversight. The report explains how police can now access massive amounts of information through data fusion software, including crime records, license plate data, facial recognition feeds, and even information about people who have never been suspected of any crime. The Brennan Center warns that these tools can embed existing biases into policing decisions and chill free speech by enabling wide-scale surveillance. The report reviews how early predictive policing programs, like the NYPD's Domain Awareness System, have combined multiple live data streams to monitor citizens in real time. While some police departments have defended these tools as more efficient, independent audits have found inaccuracies and potential misuse. The Brennan Center calls for stronger regulations and public accountability to govern how these AI tools are used in law enforcement.
