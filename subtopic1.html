Subtopic 1: Digital Forensics & Evidence

Source 1

Field Information
URL https://nij.ojp.gov/topics/forensics/digital-multimedia-evidence
Site Author National Institute of Justice (U.S. Department of Justice)
Abstract This web page from the National Institute of Justice provides an overview of how law enforcement agencies gather and use digital evidence during criminal investigations. In essence, digital evidence (data) represents any type of information stored electronically that may be used in court. Digital Evidence includes data found on computers, phones and other storage devices. According to the webpage; digital forensics (also known as computer forensics), is now considered a primary tool used by police to investigate and prosecute criminals. Law enforcement agencies are faced with the challenge of staying current with emerging technology trends and require training to properly identify and handle digital evidence. Additionally, according to the webpage, digital evidence is most often associated with Electronic Crimes such as Credit Card Fraud and Cybercrime, however; digital evidence is used in virtually every type of criminal case. The federal government encourages local law enforcement to develop enhanced capabilities for Digital Forensic Investigations across the country to assist investigators with their ability to conduct effective investigations.

Source 2

Field Information
URL https://www.nist.gov/digital-evidence
Site Author National Institute of Standards and Technology (NIST)
Abstract The National Institute of Standards and Technology (NIST) has an extensive resource on digital forensics for use in criminal investigations. This resource outlines what digital forensics is — the gathering, storage and analysis of computerized information; specifically, the retrieval of electronic information from items like computers, mobile phones and hard drive. In addition to defining this term, NIST identifies some of the major hurdles for investigators involved in these types of cases. For example, there are many times when investigators have difficulty obtaining usable data from damaged devices. Investigators may have to sift through thousands of pieces of data to find one piece of evidence. Additionally, investigators must take extreme care to ensure they do not damage or alter evidence during collection. NIST also identifies current projects that support the development of new tools and technologies for the digital forensic community. Examples include the National Software Reference Library and the Cloud Computing Forensic Science Program. Both projects provide ways to enhance the quality and reliability of tools provided to law enforcement agencies.
