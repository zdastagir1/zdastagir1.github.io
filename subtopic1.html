Subtopic 1: Digital Forensics & Evidence

Source 1

Field Information
URL https://nij.ojp.gov/topics/forensics/digital-multimedia-evidence
Site Author National Institute of Justice (U.S. Department of Justice)
Abstract This page from the National Institute of Justice explains how law enforcement agencies collect and use digital evidence as part of criminal investigations. Digital evidence refers to any information stored or transmitted in electronic form that can be used in court. This includes data found on computers, smartphones, and other storage devices. The site discusses how digital forensics, also called computer forensics, has become a core part of how police fight crime. Agencies face challenges in keeping up with rapidly changing technologies, and officers must be trained to collect and handle digital evidence properly. The page also covers how digital evidence is commonly linked to electronic crimes like credit card fraud and cybercrime, but it also plays a role in all types of criminal cases. The government emphasizes the need for law enforcement to build stronger digital forensics capability nationwide to support investigations more effectively.

Source 2

Field Information
URL https://www.nist.gov/digital-evidence
Site Author National Institute of Standards and Technology (NIST)
Abstract This website from the National Institute of Standards and Technology provides a detailed overview of digital forensics and how it is used in criminal investigations. It defines digital forensics as the process of retrieving, storing, and analyzing electronic data from devices such as computers, phones, and hard drives. NIST highlights several challenges that investigators face, including recovering data from damaged devices, identifying relevant evidence within massive amounts of data, and making sure evidence is captured without being altered. The site also describes specific NIST projects that help the forensics community, such as the National Software Reference Library and the Cloud Computing Forensic Science Program. These programs aim to improve the accuracy and reliability of digital forensic tools used by law enforcement. The page also notes that newer sources of digital evidence, such as drones and connected vehicles, are becoming increasingly important in modern investigations.
