Subtopic 2: Predictive Policing & AI

Source 1

Field Information
URL https://naacp.org/resources/artificial-intelligence-predictive-policing-issue-brief
Site Author NAACP (National Association for the Advancement of Colored People)
Abstract The National Association for the Advancement of Colored People (NAACP) issued an Issue Brief that addressed some of the same concerns as above regarding the development and deployment of Artificial Intelligence (AI) used in Predictive Policing. According to the NAACP, Predictive Policing is a type of software that utilizes crime data from the past, along with algorithms, to predict where future crimes will take place and who may commit those crimes. As such, according to the NAACP, since the systems rely upon past crime data collected by police departments; therefore, if there were or are existing racial disparities within those same police departments, then those disparities will be reflected within the predictive systems. Furthermore, the NAACP points out that the Black Community is already being subjected to disproportionate levels of "over-policing," which makes matters even worse when AI-based predictive tools are added into the equation. In response, several United States Senators have written letters to the Department of Justice asking the DOJ to discontinue its funding of any Predictive Policing Systems until adequate audit processes and/or other Due Process protections are established. Additionally, the NAACP has recommended that all states should establish laws requiring transparency in how the predictive tools operate, prohibition on utilizing biased data sets, and involvement by affected communities in decision-making as to whether to deploy AI-technology in their respective local Law Enforcement agencies.

Source 2

Field Information
URL https://www.brennancenter.org/our-work/research-reports/dangers-unregulated-ai-policing
Site Author Brennan Center for Justice
Abstract A recent report from the Brennan Center for Justice provides a detailed look at an increasing trend: police department usage of artificial intelligence (AI) enabled Data Fusion platforms; and the risks associated with utilizing such systems without adequate oversight. According to the report, once a police agency purchases a data fusion platform, they will be able to gather vast amounts of citizen data, using crime records, license plate data, facial recognition systems, and even information on individuals who have never been under suspicion of committing a crime. The Brennan Center is concerned that data fusion technology has the ability to integrate bias into the decision-making process for law enforcement agencies, and also the capability to restrict free speech by allowing large scale monitoring of citizens. Additionally, the Brennan Center highlights how initial predictive policing projects, such as New York City Police Department's (NYPD) Domain Awareness System, allow for several types of live data stream to be monitored in real-time. Some police departments have stated that such platforms allow them to become more productive. However, outside audits of their utilization have identified errors or misuses. The Brennan Center advocates for increased regulation and public accountability to help guide the appropriate implementation of AI based solutions in law enforcement.
